Sea Otter
Enhydra lutris
Endangered

The only truly marine otter, the sea otter spends almost its entire life in the ocean along the North Pacific coast. It is the heaviest member of the weasel family and one of the only mammals known to use tools — wrapping shellfish in kelp and cracking them open on a stone balanced on its chest.

Amazing Fact Sea otters have the densest fur of any animal — up to 1 million hairs per square inch. They have no insulating blubber, so their fur traps air to keep them warm. They must groom constantly to maintain this waterproof insulation.
North American River Otter
Lontra canadensis
Least Concern

The most widespread otter in North America, found from Alaska to Florida. A highly social and playful species that slides down muddy or snowy riverbanks purely for fun. Excellent swimmers capable of holding their breath for up to 8 minutes and reaching 11 km/h underwater.

Amazing Fact River otters can close their nostrils and ears when underwater. They communicate with over 12 different vocalizations including chirps, growls, screams, and a distinctive "chitter" contact call.
Giant Otter
Pteronura brasiliensis
Endangered

The world's longest otter, found in South American rivers. Giant otters are apex predators with no natural enemies — they are so fierce that jaguars and caimans avoid them. They live in tight family groups called "holts" with up to 20 members and vocalize loudly to defend territory.

Amazing Fact Each giant otter has a unique throat patch pattern — like a fingerprint — which researchers use to identify individuals in the wild. They eat up to 4 kg of fish per day and have been recorded killing and eating young caimans.
Eurasian Otter
Lutra lutra
Near Threatened

The most widely distributed otter in the world, ranging across Europe, Asia, and parts of North Africa. Primarily nocturnal and solitary, it was nearly wiped out by hunting and pesticides in the 20th century. Conservation efforts have allowed populations to recover significantly across Europe.

Amazing Fact Male Eurasian otters can have a territory stretching 40 km along a riverbank. They communicate by leaving spraints (droppings) infused with scent gland secretions at prominent landmarks — a complex chemical language.
Asian Small-clawed Otter
Aonyx cinereus
Vulnerable

The world's smallest otter species, native to South and Southeast Asia. Its most distinctive feature is its partially webbed paws with tiny vestigial claws, giving it extraordinary dexterity to feel for prey in muddy water. Lives in large family groups of up to 15 individuals, all helping raise pups.

Amazing Fact Unlike other otters that catch fish with their mouths, Asian small-clawed otters use their sensitive, nimble paws to hunt — feeling under rocks and in crevices for crabs and shellfish. They have 12 distinct vocalizations.
Smooth-coated Otter
Lutrogale perspicillata
Vulnerable

The largest otter in Asia, named for its unusually short and sleek fur compared to other species. Found across South and Southeast Asia in wetlands, rivers, and estuaries. Forms large family groups and is a cooperative hunter — packs will herd fish into shallow water before attacking together.

Amazing Fact Smooth-coated otters are the only otter species known to cooperatively herd and hunt fish. A family group will form a line and splash to drive fish into shallows, then strike simultaneously — a strategy usually seen only in dolphins and orcas.
Hairy-nosed Otter
Lutra sumatrana
Endangered

One of the rarest and least-studied otters in the world, found in Southeast Asian lowland forests and coastal areas. Named for the distinctive hair covering the upper surface of its nose — a feature unique among otters. It was believed extinct for decades until rediscovery in the 1990s in Sumatra.

Amazing Fact The hairy-nosed otter was thought to be extinct by the early 20th century. It was rediscovered in the 1990s in Vietnam and Thailand. So little is known about this species that even basic facts like its average lifespan remain undocumented.
Spotted-necked Otter
Hydrictis maculicollis
Near Threatened

A medium-sized African otter named for the distinctive cream-and-brown mottled spots on its throat and chest — each individual's pattern is unique, like a fingerprint. Found across sub-Saharan Africa in clear, clean lakes and rivers. Highly dependent on water quality and suffers when pollution increases.

Amazing Fact Lake Victoria in East Africa once held thriving populations of spotted-necked otters. The introduction of the Nile perch — which wiped out hundreds of native cichlid fish species — devastated local otter populations by collapsing their food source.
African Clawless Otter
Aonyx capensis
Least Concern

The second-largest freshwater otter species and the largest in Africa. Named for its nearly claw-free, unwebbed front paws — which give it remarkable manual dexterity for catching and holding slippery prey. Also called the "Cape clawless otter." Highly adaptable and can tolerate saltwater coastlines.

Amazing Fact African clawless otters catch crabs entirely with their hands — they dig through mud and crevices with nimble, unwebbed fingers, then crush the crab with their powerful molar teeth. They are sometimes seen "wrestling" with large crabs for several minutes.
Congo Clawless Otter
Aonyx congicus
Near Threatened

The most mysterious and least-known African otter, restricted to the dense rainforests of central Africa, particularly the Congo Basin. Sometimes considered a subspecies of the African clawless otter but recognized as a distinct species by most authorities. So elusive that most knowledge comes from museum specimens.

Amazing Fact The Congo clawless otter has smaller, paler teeth and slightly smaller claws than its African cousin — suggesting adaptation to different prey. Because it lives in some of Africa's most impenetrable jungle, scientists still debate how many exist or exactly what it eats.
Neotropical Otter
Lontra longicaudis
Near Threatened

A medium-sized otter ranging from Mexico to Argentina — the most widespread Latin American otter. Despite its large geographic range, populations are fragmented and declining due to hunting for its pelt and habitat loss. More solitary than the giant otter and primarily nocturnal and crepuscular.

Amazing Fact Neotropical otters are exceptionally adaptable — they've been documented hunting in flooded forests, mangroves, beaches, and mountain streams up to 3,000 m elevation. They also eat a surprising variety of prey including insects, birds, and small reptiles.
Southern River Otter
Lontra provocax
Endangered

One of South America's most critically threatened mammals, the southern river otter is restricted to parts of Chile and Argentina. It can inhabit both freshwater rivers and marine coastal areas — one of the few freshwater otters to regularly use saltwater habitats. Hunted almost to extinction by fur traders in the 20th century.

Amazing Fact In Patagonia, southern river otters sometimes hunt in the ocean alongside kelp beds — a behavior more like sea otters than river otters. Their population is so small and fragmented that inbreeding is now a serious conservation concern.
Marine Otter
Lontra felina
Endangered

Also called the "sea cat" (gato marino) in Spanish, the marine otter is the only otter classified in the genus Lontra (river otters) to live primarily in the ocean. It hugs rocky Pacific coastlines of Peru and Chile, foraging in turbulent surf zones. It is the rarest otter in the Americas and possibly on Earth.

Amazing Fact The marine otter is unique — it is the only river otter lineage that evolved to live in the open ocean. Unlike sea otters, it sleeps on rocky shores rather than floating at sea. It's so small that it hunts in the crashing surf, clinging to rocks with its claws to avoid being swept away.
